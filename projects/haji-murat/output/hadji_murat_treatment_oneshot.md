# HADJI MURAD

## Feature Film Adaptation Treatment

*Based solely on Leo Tolstoy’s novel* **Hadji Murad**

---

## 1. LOGLINE

A legendary Caucasian warrior, betrayed by Imam Shamil and distrusted by the Russian Empire, surrenders to his enemies in a desperate attempt to save his captive family. But as both powers use him as a political instrument, his only remaining freedom becomes a doomed escape toward the mountains.

## 2. GENRE

Historical war drama.  
Subgenre: tragic political captivity drama / anti-imperial character study.

## 3. FORMAT & RUNTIME

Feature film.  
Intended runtime: 150 minutes.

## 4. PRINCIPAL CHARACTERS

### HADJI MURAD (40s)

A famed Avar commander, once Shamil’s most feared naib, now a fugitive between two systems of power. His motivation is precise: survive long enough to rescue his mother, wives, and children from Shamil, then reclaim honor through vengeance. His arc is not from ignorance to knowledge but from tactical confidence to existential clarity. He begins believing he can use the Russians as tools against Shamil; he ends understanding that both Shamil and the Russians have made him a hostage. His final act restores agency through death rather than victory.

### SHAMIL (50s)

The Imam of the Caucasian resistance, austere, theatrical, politically ruthless. He is not merely Hadji Murad’s enemy; he is Hadji Murad’s mirror: another man of faith, command, pride, and calculation. His motivation is to preserve authority by making Hadji Murad’s defection unbearable. His visible arc is one of tightening control: from distant threat to domestic tyrant, from religious ruler to man capable of blinding a rival’s son to protect his symbolic power.

### YUSUF (18)

Hadji Murad’s son, imprisoned by Shamil in a pit and threatened with blinding. He represents Hadji Murad’s exposed heart: youth, lineage, masculine promise, and the future Hadji Murad cannot protect from a distance. His arc is brief but essential. He tries to remain proud under interrogation, then collapses into a suicide attempt when he learns the shape of Shamil’s mercy. He is the living stake of the film.

### PATIMAT (70s)

Hadji Murad’s mother, held with his wives and grandchildren in Vedeno. She is not sentimentalized; she is severe, old, and bound to honor. Her presence externalizes Hadji Murad’s obligation to blood and household. She carries the ancestral judgment under which Hadji Murad acts.

### SOFIAT (30s)

Hadji Murad’s wife and mother of some of his children. She functions as the emotional center of the captive family. Her motivation is survival without dishonor. Her arc is mostly silent: from waiting, to dread, to the knowledge that men’s political vows are being paid for with women and children’s bodies.

### ELDAR (late teens / early 20s)

Hadji Murad’s young murid: beautiful, disciplined, physically powerful, almost filial. His motivation is absolute loyalty. He admires Hadji Murad with the intensity of a young warrior who has not yet learned the cost of legend. His arc moves from silent obedience to impatient battle-fire; he dies trying to turn captivity into a charge.

### KHAN MAHOMA (20s)

Hadji Murad’s cheerful henchman, lively, charming, and reckless. He brings warmth and human texture to Hadji Murad’s band. His motivation is loyalty mixed with appetite for life. His arc darkens as his joking vitality is carried into the final ditch, where song and gunfire become indistinguishable.

### GAMZALO (30s/40s)

A one-eyed Chechen murid, red-haired, scarred, openly hostile to Russians. He does not disguise hatred and therefore becomes the clearest moral opposite to the courtly diplomacy around Hadji Murad. His motivation is simple: never submit inwardly. His arc is static by design; he remains the film’s hard mineral core of resistance.

### SADO (40s)

A Chechen host who shelters Hadji Murad despite Shamil’s death order. His motivation is the sacred obligation of hospitality. His arc is tragic: an honorable private act brings imperial violence upon his household. Through him the film shows that political history destroys not only commanders, but roofs, orchards, bees, sons, and water.

### PRINCE SIMON VORONTSOV (30s)

Young Russian commander, refined, ambitious, socially graceful, and thrilled by the prestige of receiving Hadji Murad’s surrender. His motivation is career, family honor, and romanticized command. His arc is exposure: what he sees as a brilliant military episode is revealed as one more elegant surface over coercion.

### PRINCESS MARYA VASILEVNA (30s)

Simon Vorontsov’s beautiful wife, socially confident, flirtatious, and fascinated by Hadji Murad as an exotic warrior guest. Her motivation is stimulation inside fortress monotony. Her arc is limited but pointed: she recognizes charm and nobility in Hadji Murad but never has to pay the cost of that recognition.

### VICEROY MIKHAIL VORONTSOV (70s)

The elder Vorontsov, Commander-in-Chief in the Caucasus. Urbane, elderly, polished, and accustomed to power. His motivation is strategic advantage without disorder. His arc is bureaucratic evasion: he understands Hadji Murad’s need, promises assistance, and delays until delay becomes fatal.

### LORIS-MELIKOV (30s)

An aide-de-camp fluent in Tartar, tasked with drawing out Hadji Murad’s history. His motivation is service to the Viceroy, but also curiosity. He becomes the film’s controlled listener, allowing Hadji Murad’s past to surface in cinematic fragments. His arc is from recorder of information to witness of a man trapped by what others reduce to intelligence.

### PETER AVDEEV (20s)

A Russian soldier who enlisted in place of his brother. His motivation is humble duty mixed with homesickness and regret. His death is structurally crucial: he prevents the film from turning Russians into abstractions. His arc runs from bored night watch, to accidental casualty, to a death that echoes in a peasant household far from the Caucasus.

### BUTLER (20s)

A young Russian officer intoxicated by the romance of Caucasian war. He befriends Hadji Murad, admires him, gambles, drinks, and gradually decays. His motivation is self-dramatization. His arc is moral disillusionment resisted until the final sight of Hadji Murad’s severed head forces the truth of war into the room.

### MARYA DMITRIEVNA (30)

Major Petrov’s companion, earthy, direct, practical, and morally clearer than the officers around her. She respects Hadji Murad instinctively and condemns the grotesque handling of his corpse. Her motivation is decency without ideology. Her arc turns her from domestic observer into the film’s plainest moral voice.

### EMPEROR NICHOLAS I (50s)

Remote imperial authority, convinced of his own strategic genius. His motivation is domination, order, and self-image. He never meets Hadji Murad, which is the point: his commands shape lives he does not see. His arc is anti-arc; he remains enclosed in ceremony while violence travels outward from his desk.

## 5. SYNOPSIS

The film opens in a summer field, where an unnamed traveler tries to pluck a crimson thistle and finds it almost impossible to tear from the earth. Later, in a ploughed field where nearly everything living has been destroyed, he sees another thistle crushed by a cartwheel yet still standing. The image calls forth the story of Hadji Murad.

In late 1851, Hadji Murad, once Shamil’s most dangerous commander, rides secretly into a hostile Chechen village with only one murid. Shamil has declared him a traitor and ordered him seized alive or dead. Sado, bound by the ancient law of hospitality, shelters him, feeds him, and arranges contact with the Russians. At the nearby Russian fort, common soldiers keep night watch, smoke illegally, and talk of homesickness. Hadji Murad’s envoy reaches Prince Simon Vorontsov, who is delighted by the prospect of receiving a legendary enemy.

Before dawn, Hadji Murad’s hiding place is exposed. Armed villagers try to stop him, but he rides through them and escapes to his waiting murids in the forest. At daybreak, during a Russian wood-felling expedition, Hadji Murad appears before Vorontsov and surrenders to the Tsar. The Russians stare at the man they have feared for years. His smile disarms some of them; his one-eyed murid Gamzalo terrifies them. A skirmish linked to the meeting wounds the soldier Avdeev, who later dies in hospital, while his peasant family far away continues its hard winter labor unaware that the son who took his brother’s place is gone.

Hadji Murad is received as a guest and prize. Young Vorontsov and his wife treat him with a mixture of courtesy, fascination, and vanity. A senior Russian general quarrels over protocol, and Hadji Murad, though not understanding every word, understands the deeper truth: he matters to them. Sent to Tiflis, he meets the elder Viceroy Vorontsov and offers to help destroy Shamil if the Russians will rescue his family. He tells his life story to Loris-Melikov: the murder of khans, his own early shame, his rise under Shamil, his rivalry with him, and the betrayal that left his mother, wives, and son in Shamil’s hands.

The Russian system turns Hadji Murad’s crisis into correspondence. Vorontsov writes to Petersburg. Minister Chernyshov reframes the matter for Emperor Nicholas. Nicholas, enclosed in court routine and vanity, orders harsher measures against the Chechens. The result is a raid. Russian troops destroy the same village where Sado sheltered Hadji Murad. Sado returns to a ruined home, dead son, polluted fountain, burned bees, and desecrated mosque. The private moral law of hospitality is answered by the machinery of empire.

Meanwhile, Shamil holds Hadji Murad’s family in Vedeno. Yusuf is dragged from a pit and interrogated. Shamil chooses not to kill him yet, but threatens to blind him. Hadji Murad, hearing reports through spies, grows restless. Among Russians he is treated well enough to be displayed but not trusted enough to be helped. At a fort, he forms a strange friendship with Butler and earns the respect of Marya Dmitrievna. He gives gifts according to the custom of the kunak, survives an assassination attempt by a blood enemy, and leaves behind admiration mixed with suspicion.

Time closes around him. He petitions Vorontsov daily for a prisoner exchange; Vorontsov delays. Moved to Nukha, closer to Muslim life but still guarded, Hadji Murad receives word that Shamil may destroy his family before any negotiation succeeds. He weighs return to Shamil, service to Russia, or flight. The fable of the falcon with bells tells him the truth: after living among men, a falcon cannot simply return to the mountains. He decides to escape anyway.

On April 25, Hadji Murad rides out with all five henchmen and a reduced Cossack escort. He increases speed. When Nazarov tries to stop him, Hadji Murad shoots him and the murids cut down the others. One Cossack escapes, raising the alarm. Hadji Murad turns away from the obvious road to the mountains, trying to outthink pursuit, but flooded rice fields trap the horses. He and his men take refuge in a clump of shrubs, dig into an old ditch, and prepare to fight.

At dawn, militia surrounds them. Hadji Murad refuses surrender with gunfire. The battle lasts until ammunition and blood run out. Bata is wounded. Eldar dies at Hadji Murad’s feet. Hadji Murad is hit in the side and knows the wound is fatal. Images pass through him: an old warrior with a severed cheek, Vorontsov’s pale cunning face, Yusuf, Sofiat, Shamil. His body continues after his hopes end. He rises, fires, kills one more attacker, and walks toward the enemy with a dagger. He is shot down, rises once more against a tree, terrifying the men rushing him, then falls like a cut thistle.

His enemies behead him. Later, Russian officers gather around the severed head with curiosity and drunken admiration. Marya Dmitrievna condemns them as cut-throats. The film returns to the crushed thistle in the ploughed field: broken, mutilated, still refusing submission.

## 6. TREATMENT

### ACT I — THE FUGITIVE AND THE GUEST

#### 1. FIELD / RUSSIAN COUNTRYSIDE — DAY

An unnamed traveler walks home after hay harvest, gathering delicate summer flowers. He sees a crimson thistle in a ditch and tries to pluck it for his bouquet. The plant resists him with thorns and fibers until he destroys its beauty in the act of taking it. Function: establishes the central image of violent possession ruining what it admires.

#### 2. PLOUGHED FIELD — LATER

The traveler crosses a black field where every living thing has been cut under by human labor. A crushed thistle stands alone, broken, muddied, twisted, yet upright. The traveler studies it and remembers Hadji Murad. Function: frame device; the thistle becomes the film’s visual thesis.

#### 3. CHECHEN AOUL OF MAKHMET — NOVEMBER EVENING

Hadji Murad rides into the village wrapped in hood and burka, reduced from famous commander to fugitive. Only Eldar follows him. Smoke, cattle, women at the fountain, and the final sound of the muezzin establish a world of communal life under threat. Function: introduces Hadji Murad through stealth, not exposition.

#### 4. SADO’S ROOF AND COURTYARD — CONTINUOUS

Hadji Murad wakes the old grandfather sleeping under a sheepskin. Recognition passes through the old man’s face. He leads Hadji Murad inside with the careful ceremony of hospitality. Function: shows Hadji Murad’s status among mountain people and the sacred code that protects him.

#### 5. SADO’S GUEST ROOM — NIGHT

Sado’s wife prepares cushions. Weapons are hung beside the household arms. Hadji Murad and the old man pray. Eldar enters and sits rigidly, armed and silent. Function: externalizes trust through ritual objects: cushions, weapons, prayer, water.

#### 6. SADO ENTERS — NIGHT

Sado arrives from the mosque and warns that Shamil has ordered Hadji Murad seized alive or dead. He promises that no one will harm a guest under his roof while his head remains on his shoulders, but he warns that open ground is another matter. Function: states the immediate danger and Sado’s moral position.

#### 7. SADO’S ROOM — LATER

Bata, a quick, sun-blackened Chechen, is brought in to guide Hadji Murad’s envoy to the Russians. Hadji Murad gives exact instructions with almost no wasted words. He also gives Sado a hidden note for his son. Women enter with tea, bread, cheese, honey, and water; the men fall silent until they leave. Function: sets up the Russian contact and Hadji Murad’s family stake.

#### 8. RUSSIAN OUTPOST NEAR VOZDVIZHENSK — SAME NIGHT

Panov, Avdeev, Nikitin, and Bondarenko take position by a broken plane tree. They make an improvised pipe in the ground and smoke though it is forbidden. Their talk drifts from company money to homesickness. Avdeev reveals that he enlisted in place of his brother and now sometimes regrets it. Function: humanizes the Russian rank-and-file before they become instruments of the war.

#### 9. OUTPOST ROAD — LATER

Bata and Khan Mahoma approach through the leaves. The soldiers challenge them. Bata declares they are friendly and need Prince Vorontsov. Avdeev and Bondarenko escort them in. Avdeev tries to talk with the Chechens and sees them as men like himself. Function: first crossing between Hadji Murad’s world and the Russian world.

#### 10. VORONTSOV HOUSE / DRAWING ROOM — MIDNIGHT

Prince Simon Vorontsov, Princess Marya Vasilevna, Poltoratsky, the adjutant, and guests play cards in candlelit luxury. Marya’s beauty distracts Poltoratsky into mistakes. A servant summons Vorontsov away. Function: contrasts fortress privilege with the soldiers outside and the fugitive in the village.

#### 11. VORONTSOV HOUSE / STUDY AND DRAWING ROOM — NIGHT

Vorontsov receives the envoys and returns animated, ordering champagne. He hints that something important will happen at the next day’s wood-felling expedition. Marya Vasilevna guesses Hadji Murad is involved and draws the truth from him. Function: positions Hadji Murad as military prize and social excitement.

#### 12. POLTORATSKY’S QUARTERS — NIGHT

Poltoratsky returns intoxicated by Marya Vasilevna’s attention and bullies his drunken servant Vovilo. Vovilo, half asleep, reveals through behavior the misery of domestic servitude. Function: shows Russian hierarchy reproducing domination at every level.

#### 13. SADO’S GUEST ROOM — DEEP NIGHT

Hadji Murad sleeps fully dressed with pistol and dagger. Sado wakes him: a woman has seen him arrive; the elders have gathered at the mosque; the village may detain him to appease Shamil. Function: turns hospitality into immediate flight.

#### 14. SADO’S COURTYARD AND VILLAGE LANES — PRE-DAWN

The black-eyed boy brings horses. Hadji Murad thanks Sado, mounts, and rides down the narrow lane. Men try to block him. Hadji Murad draws his pistol and rides straight at them. They scatter. Shots crack behind him and miss. Function: first visible proof of Hadji Murad’s nerve.

#### 15. RAVINE AND FOREST EDGE — PRE-DAWN

Mounted villagers pursue, needing to appear loyal to Shamil. Hadji Murad stops, draws his rifle, and dares them to take him. They hold back. He fires once and disappears toward the forest. Function: shows how reputation functions as armor.

#### 16. FOREST GLADE — BEFORE DAWN

Hadji Murad reaches Khanefi, Gamzalo, Khan Mahoma, Bata, and the other men. The fire is stamped out. Hadji Murad prays alone after ablution. Then he imagines returning with Russian troops, seizing Shamil, and hearing Shamil’s wives cry. Jackals transform the dream into mockery. Function: reveals Hadji Murad’s plan and the revenge fantasy beneath it.

#### 17. FOREST GLADE — DAWN

Khan Mahoma returns from Vorontsov with news that the prince will receive Hadji Murad as a guest at the Shalin glade. Hadji Murad pays Bata, orders weapons cleaned and garments arranged. Function: shifts from fugitive survival to formal surrender.

#### 18. SHALIN WOODS / RUSSIAN FELLING LINE — MORNING

Russian soldiers fell trees in mist. Officers eat, drink, and joke on drums. A shot from across the ravine triggers an unnecessary exchange. Poltoratsky encourages the firing as if arranging sport. Function: establishes war as boredom seeking spectacle.

#### 19. RAVINE EDGE — MORNING

Avdeev is hit in the stomach during the pointless firing. He asks for vodka but cannot drink it. Panov drinks it instead. Avdeev is carried away on a cloak. Function: first blood cost of the surrender episode, paid by an ordinary soldier.

#### 20. WOOD ROAD — MORNING

Vorontsov arrives and tells Poltoratsky that Hadji Murad has surrendered. Hadji Murad appears on a white-maned horse, gold weapons visible, his murids behind him. He greets Vorontsov and gives himself to the will of the Russian Tsar through the interpreter. Function: Act I public turning point; the fugitive becomes guest-prisoner.

#### 21. WOOD ROAD / SOLDIERS’ LINE — CONTINUOUS

The Russian soldiers gather to stare. They remember him as killer and enemy, but some admire his bearing. Gamzalo’s one-eyed hatred unsettles them. Hadji Murad understands they are speaking of him and smiles with his eyes. Function: complicates enemy image; creates charisma without speech.

#### 22. VORONTSOV HOUSE — DAY

Marya Vasilevna and her young son meet Hadji Murad. Hadji Murad treats the household as sacred because the prince has received him as kunak. The child admires his dagger, and Hadji Murad gives it to him. Function: hospitality is mirrored inside the Russian house, but with social vanity beneath it.

#### 23. HADJI MURAD’S ASSIGNED ROOM — DAY

Alone, Hadji Murad’s pleasant mask disappears. He checks whether his men have kept their weapons, whether horses are watched, and whether he may be seized. He prays and keeps his dagger near. Function: externalizes distrust; the guest room is also a cell.

#### 24. VORONTSOV DINING ROOM — AFTERNOON

Hadji Murad barely eats and chooses food from where Marya Vasilevna has already served herself, revealing fear of poison. Vorontsov gives him a repeating watch after Hadji Murad admires it. Function: the watch becomes a recurring symbol of Russian gift, time, and captivity.

#### 25. MELLER-ZAKOMELSKY’S HOUSE — EVENING

General Meller-Zakomelsky angrily challenges young Vorontsov for bypassing command protocol. Marya Vasilevna intervenes socially and dissolves the quarrel. In the waiting room Hadji Murad understands that Russian officials are fighting over him and that this gives him leverage. Function: shows Russian power as vanity, hierarchy, and performance.

#### 26. HOSPITAL WARD — NIGHT

Avdeev is placed among sick and wounded men. The doctor probes his wound and notices old flogging scars. Avdeev endures pain, then seems astonished by the room around him. Function: war’s romance is stripped into flesh, scars, and institutional indifference.

#### 27. AVDEEV FAMILY FARM — WINTER DAY

Far away, Avdeev’s father, sister-in-law, and niece thresh oats in bitter cold. The household continues under practical necessity. The brother for whom Avdeev enlisted lives because Avdeev is absent. Function: expands the casualty beyond the battlefield.

#### 28. HOSPITAL WARD / FARM CROSSCUT — DAY

Avdeev dies quietly while his family’s labor continues. The film holds on the gap between imperial war and peasant knowledge. Function: closes Avdeev’s mini-arc and prevents the film from becoming only about great men.

### ACT II — THE TWO EMPIRES

#### 29. TIFLIS / VICEROY PALACE — EVENING

Elder Vorontsov receives the report of Hadji Murad’s surrender during a glittering dinner. He turns military news into refined conversation. Guests ask about Hadji Murad as if asking about an exotic animal. Function: introduces the imperial center of the Caucasus.

#### 30. TIFLIS WAITING ROOM — NEXT DAY

Hadji Murad waits among petitioners: a widow, accused officers, merchants, princes, and men seeking favor. He refuses to sit, stands with hand near dagger, and surveys the room with contempt. Function: places him inside bureaucracy and shows his refusal to become one more supplicant.

#### 31. VICEROY’S STUDY — DAY

Hadji Murad meets elder Vorontsov. Through the interpreter he offers service against Shamil and explains that Shamil holds his family. Vorontsov promises imperial mercy and pleasant treatment while inwardly distrusting him. Hadji Murad understands the distrust but continues the performance. Function: central diplomatic scene; both men read the truth beneath words.

#### 32. TIFLIS OPERA — NIGHT

Hadji Murad sits through the first act with formal indifference while the audience watches him more than the stage. He leaves early. Function: visualizes cultural captivity; he becomes spectacle.

#### 33. VORONTSOV BALL — NIGHT

Women dance in low-cut gowns, officers drink champagne, and the old Viceroy’s wife offers polite words through an interpreter. Hadji Murad tries to raise the subject of his family, but Vorontsov avoids business in public. Function: shows the cruelty of etiquette when urgent life is at stake.

#### 34. HADJI MURAD’S QUARTERS / TIFLIS — DAY

Loris-Melikov visits to take Hadji Murad’s account for the Viceroy and the Emperor. Hadji Murad is flattered that his story may reach the Tsar, but he remains controlled. Function: opens the film’s backstory mechanism.

#### 35. FLASHBACK — AVAR MOUNTAINS / KHANATE — DAY

Fragments of Hadji Murad’s youth unfold as Loris listens: khans murdered, palace violence, Abu Nutsal fighting with his cheek cut open, young Hadji Murad fleeing in fear. Function: reveals the shame that forged Hadji Murad’s fearlessness.

#### 36. HADJI MURAD’S QUARTERS — DAY

Hadji Murad admits that after that shame he feared nothing. Loris watches the older warrior’s face darken with memory. Function: turns legend into psychology without interior narration.

#### 37. FLASHBACK — KHUNZAKH / MOUNTAIN AMBUSHES — DAY AND NIGHT

Hadji Murad and his brother seek revenge, navigate Shamil’s rise, and become entangled with shifting allegiances. The film shows sharp fragments: a body falling, a horse turning, a hand on a dagger, a village changing banners. Function: compresses complex history into visual causality.

#### 38. HADJI MURAD’S QUARTERS / MURIDS’ ROOM — DAY

During a prayer break, Loris observes the murids: Gamzalo plaiting a bridle and openly hating Russians, Khan Mahoma joking, Eldar polishing gear, Khanefi working with heavy silence. Function: individualizes the band and sets their final deaths.

#### 39. HADJI MURAD’S QUARTERS — LATER

Hadji Murad resumes his story. He explains how Shamil feared him, stripped his authority, seized his property, demanded obedience, and trapped his family. He concludes that he is tied by a rope whose end is in Shamil’s hands. Function: states the protagonist’s central bind.

#### 40. VICEROY’S OFFICE — DECEMBER

Vorontsov writes to Minister Chernyshov, presenting Hadji Murad as a possible asset but emphasizing the family problem. The letter is elegant, reasonable, and slow. Function: transforms urgent human crisis into administrative paper.

#### 41. IMPERIAL COURIER ROAD — WINTER

A courier overdrives horses and abuses drivers to carry the report toward Petersburg. Function: visualizes the violence hidden inside bureaucracy’s speed.

#### 42. PETERSBURG / MINISTER CHERNYSHOV’S OFFICE — NEW YEAR

Chernyshov reads Vorontsov’s report with political malice. He plans to undercut Vorontsov while appearing loyal to the Emperor. Function: introduces imperial distortion.

#### 43. WINTER PALACE / EMPEROR’S ROUTINE — DAY

Nicholas moves through family, ceremony, self-importance, and strategic certainty. He treats the Caucasus as a map problem. Function: shows the distant source of orders without making him omniscient.

#### 44. WINTER PALACE / REPORT AUDIENCE — DAY

Chernyshov presents Hadji Murad as dangerous and recommends suspicion. Nicholas confirms that pressure on the Chechens must intensify: forests felled, cordons tightened, raids continued. Function: imperial midpoint; Hadji Murad’s surrender leads not to peace but harsher war.

#### 45. CHECHNYA / RUSSIAN RAID COLUMN — JANUARY DAY

Russian troops move silently through winter terrain with infantry, Cossacks, and guns. A gazelle bursts from brush and soldiers briefly become boys chasing it. The column then resumes destruction. Function: juxtaposes natural life, childish impulse, and organized violence.

#### 46. CHECHEN AOUL — DAY

The raid burns hay, smashes orchards, pollutes the fountain and mosque, kills livestock, and wrecks homes. Soldiers take food and objects; officers talk of routine. Function: externalizes Nicholas’s order as lived atrocity.

#### 47. SADO’S RUINED SAKLYA — AFTERMATH

Sado returns to find his house destroyed. His son, the bright-eyed boy who admired Hadji Murad, is brought dead to the mosque, stabbed in the back. His wife tears at her own face. The old grandfather sees burned bees and broken trees. Function: pays off Sado’s hospitality and turns political war into household grief.

#### 48. AOUL MOSQUE AND FOUNTAIN — DAY

The villagers clean the desecrated mosque and polluted water. They do not speak of hatred but move with repulsion deeper than hatred. The elders decide to send envoys to Shamil for help. Function: shows how Russian violence strengthens the very resistance it means to crush.

#### 49. RUSSIAN FORT / BUTLER’S MORNING WALK — AFTER RAID

Butler walks in beautiful mountain light, pleased with his own bravery and the romance of war. He remembers Marya Dmitrievna’s kindness at dinner. Function: introduces Butler as the self-deceiving romantic observer.

#### 50. MAJOR PETROV’S HOUSE — DAY

Hadji Murad arrives at the major’s house with selected murids. Major Petrov is coarse and drunk-friendly; Marya Dmitrievna is practical, curious, and respectful. Butler is fascinated. Function: creates the domestic Russian space where Hadji Murad is seen more humanly.

#### 51. PETROV HOUSE / HADJI MURAD’S ROOM — DAYS PASSING

Spies come and go. Hadji Murad receives bad news about his family. He talks with Butler through interpreter, gesture, and smiles. Marya Dmitrievna tracks every report about his wives, children, and son. Function: builds intimacy and pressure.

#### 52. VEDENO / FAMILY PRISON — DAY

Hadji Murad’s mother, wives, and children are guarded in Ibrahim Raschid’s house. Yusuf sits in a deep pit with criminals. The family waits for Shamil’s decision. Function: reveals the stakes directly rather than through reports.

#### 53. VEDENO / SHAMIL’S RETURN — DAY

Shamil returns from battle surrounded by murids performing victory. He enters with authority, piety, and theatrical restraint. Function: introduces Shamil physically as rival sovereign.

#### 54. SHAMIL’S HOUSE / FAMILY QUARTERS — DAY

Shamil’s domestic world appears: wives, jealousies, controlled movements, guarded women. His public austerity is contrasted with private vanity and household politics. Function: complicates Shamil without excusing him.

#### 55. SHAMIL’S AUDIENCE ROOM — DAY

Yusuf is brought from the pit. Shamil questions him about his father. Yusuf holds himself with pride. Shamil declares that he will not kill him yet but will put out his eyes as punishment for treachery. Function: turns the family stake into an irreversible ticking clock.

#### 56. PRISON PIT — DUSK

After leaving Shamil, Yusuf breaks. He seizes a dagger and tries to kill himself, but guards stop him and return him to the pit. Function: shows the psychic violence of threatened mutilation.

#### 57. PETROV HOUSE — DAY

A spy’s report reaches Hadji Murad. He receives the news without display, but his body changes: no appetite, no sleep, prayer repeated, sudden movement to the door. Function: externalizes inner panic through discipline cracking.

#### 58. PETROV HOUSE / DINING ROOM — DAY

Marya Dmitrievna praises Hadji Murad’s white burka. By kunak custom, he gives it to her. He gives Major Petrov a valuable sword. Petrov offers a horse in return; Hadji Murad refuses and gestures that his heart is in the mountains. Function: gift exchange becomes farewell and confession.

#### 59. PETROV HOUSE / PORCH — CONTINUOUS

Arslan Khan rides up and fires at Hadji Murad because of a blood feud. Hadji Murad reacts like a cat, rushes him despite his limp, seizes the bridle, and draws his dagger before others separate them. Function: shows that even under Russian protection, old mountain debts can erupt instantly.

#### 60. PETROV HOUSE / AFTER DEPARTURE — DAY

Officers debate whether Hadji Murad is noble or treacherous. Marya Dmitrievna defends him sharply, saying he is courteous, wise, and just. Butler agrees. Function: places moral perception in characters outside official power.

#### 61. RUSSIAN CAMP / CHECHEN LINE — WEEKS LATER

Life at the forts resumes: alarms, raids, stolen horses, expected expeditions. Baryatinsky arrives. Young Vorontsov lives in splendid tented comfort, while Marya Vasilevna visits camp. Function: shows war continuing as aristocratic lifestyle.

#### 62. BUTLER’S QUARTERS / FORT — NIGHT

Butler gambles, loses heavily, borrows at ruinous terms, drinks more, and tries to preserve his romantic self-image. His courtship of Marya Dmitrievna becomes crude and is rejected. Function: darkens Butler into moral weakness before the final revelation.

### ACT III — THE LAST FREEDOM

#### 63. TIFLIS / VICEROY’S PALACE — DAY

Hadji Murad returns from Chechnya without achieving his aim. He petitions Vorontsov daily to collect mountaineer prisoners and exchange them for his family. Vorontsov promises vaguely and delays. Function: turns Russian hospitality into paralysis.

#### 64. TIFLIS / PALACE CORRIDORS — DAYS PASSING

Hadji Murad waits in corridors while papers move slowly. He watches doors open for lesser matters while his family remains in Shamil’s hands. Function: externalizes captivity through waiting rather than chains.

#### 65. NUKHA / ASSIGNED HOUSE NEAR MOSQUE — DAY

Hadji Murad is allowed to move to Nukha. The town’s mosque and Muslim life offer him more comfort, but officers, interpreter, and guards occupy the same house. He rides only with Cossack escort. Function: creates the final cage: culturally familiar, politically controlled.

#### 66. NUKHA HOUSE — DAY

Councillor Kirillov arrives from Tiflis with money and an order for Hadji Murad to return later to meet General Argutinski. Hadji Murad is insulted by Kirillov’s manner, takes the money, and humiliates him with sudden physical contempt. Function: shows Hadji Murad’s patience nearing its limit.

#### 67. NUKHA HOUSE / NIGHT

A messenger brings word that Shamil may soon move the family and that Avar supporters might rescue them only on the road. Hadji Murad sends a promise of three thousand rubles for their liberation. Function: creates the last possible rescue plan.

#### 68. NUKHA HOUSE / LATE NIGHT

Hadji Murad lies awake. He weighs returning to Shamil, serving Russia, or fleeing. He remembers the fable of a falcon captured by men and rejected by other falcons because of bells on its jesses. He understands that he cannot truly return, yet he cannot remain. Function: internal decision translated into fable and sleepless movement.

#### 69. HENCHMEN’S ROOM — AFTER MIDNIGHT

Hadji Murad wakes Gamzalo, Khan Mahoma, Eldar, Bata, and Khanefi. He tells them they will leave the next day under cover of a ride. Each man responds according to nature: Gamzalo with fierce readiness, Khan Mahoma with brightness, Eldar with silent devotion, Khanefi with practical attention. Function: assembles the death company.

#### 70. HADJI MURAD’S ROOM — PRE-DAWN

Hadji Murad remembers the last time he saw Yusuf: the young man wanting to accompany him, being told to protect mother and grandmother, then turning back at the stream. The memory breaks Hadji Murad’s stillness. He calls Eldar and orders the horses saddled for a ride. Function: emotional trigger for the final act.

#### 71. NUKHA STABLE YARD — MORNING, APRIL 25

Hadji Murad mounts with all five henchmen. The commander notices that he is taking too many but fails to insist. Nazarov and four Cossacks accompany them. Function: institutional laxity opens the escape.

#### 72. ROAD OUTSIDE NUKHA — MORNING

Hadji Murad gradually increases the pace. Nazarov warns him to slow. Hadji Murad does not answer. The ride becomes a chase. Function: tension escalates through speed and silence.

#### 73. ROAD / OPEN COUNTRY — CONTINUOUS

Nazarov draws level and reaches for Hadji Murad’s bridle. Hadji Murad shoots him. The murids attack the Cossacks with pistols and swords. Ignatov and Petrakov fall; Mishkin escapes toward the fortress. Hadji Murad prevents looting and drives forward. Function: point of no return; Hadji Murad chooses rescue over Russian protection.

#### 74. NUKHA FORT / ALARM — DAY

Mishkin reports the escape. The commander panics. Cossacks and militia are sent out, and a reward is offered for Hadji Murad alive or dead. Function: the Russian cage becomes a hunt.

#### 75. ROAD AND RICE FIELDS — AFTERNOON

Hadji Murad chooses the unexpected route away from the mountains, intending to cross the river and double back. Flooded rice fields trap the horses. Mud sucks at hooves. Time is lost. Function: fate enters through terrain, not coincidence.

#### 76. SHRUB CLUMP NEAR THE ALAZAN — DUSK

Hadji Murad leads the men into higher ground among shrubs. They eat bread and cheese, rest horses, and listen to nightingales. The birds remind him of the song of Hamzad and the death he may now share. Function: calm before final enclosure; nature becomes witness.

#### 77. SHRUB CLUMP — NIGHT

Karganov and militia surround the clump after an old man reveals seeing six horsemen. Hadji Murad understands they are trapped. He finds an old ditch and orders the men to dig in with daggers and branches. He works beside them. Function: final defensive position; the thistle roots itself.

#### 78. DITCH / SHRUB CLUMP — DAWN

Karganov calls for surrender. Hadji Murad answers with a shot. Militia fire from distance. Hadji Murad and his men shoot only when attackers expose themselves. Function: opens the final battle with tactical discipline.

#### 79. DITCH — MORNING

The first hour passes. Hadji Murad considers mounting a breakout toward the river, but more men arrive under Hadji Aga, once his kunak and now with the Russians. Akhmet Khan, son of an old enemy, is among them. Function: betrayal and blood feud close the circle.

#### 80. DITCH — LATER

Hadji Aga orders a sword rush. The attackers move shrub to shrub. Bata is wounded in the neck. Khanefi keeps loading rifles. Khan Mahoma sings and fires. Gamzalo exults whenever he hits. Eldar repeatedly asks to charge but is held back. Function: each murid’s essence is revealed under fire.

#### 81. DITCH — LATER

Eldar is shot and falls onto Hadji Murad’s leg. His eyes remain fixed on his master. Hadji Murad draws his leg free and keeps firing. Khanefi strips ammunition from Eldar’s body. Function: loyalty becomes material sacrifice.

#### 82. DITCH — FINAL MOMENTS

Hadji Murad is hit in the side and knows the wound is fatal. Images pass through him: Abu Nutsal with the severed cheek, old Vorontsov’s cunning face, Yusuf, Sofiat, Shamil. The images evoke no action now; his body continues without hope. Function: subjective climax without voiceover.

#### 83. SHRUB CLUMP / OPEN GROUND — CONTINUOUS

Hadji Murad rises, fires his pistol, kills one more attacker, and limps forward with dagger in hand. Shots strike him. He falls, then rises against a tree, terrifying the men rushing him. He shudders and falls face down like a mown thistle. Function: protagonist’s final agency and visual payoff of the opening image.

#### 84. SHRUB CLUMP — AFTERMATH

Hadji Aga strikes Hadji Murad’s head with a dagger, then cuts it off carefully to avoid soiling his shoes. Men gather around the bodies like hunters around slaughtered game. Nightingales resume singing. Function: moral horror; nature continues.

#### 85. RUSSIAN FORT / OFFICERS’ ROOM — DAY

A Cossack brings Hadji Murad’s severed head in a sack. Officers gather, drunk and curious. Major Petrov wants to kiss the head because Hadji Murad gave him a sword. Butler looks, unable to reconcile romance with the object on the table. Function: collapses the poetry of war into grotesque trophy.

#### 86. FORT PORCH — CONTINUOUS

Marya Dmitrievna sits outside, furious. She condemns the officers as cut-throats and rejects the excuse that this is war. Butler tries to answer but has no real answer. Function: final moral judgment.

#### 87. PLOUGHED FIELD — DAY

The film returns to the traveler and the crushed thistle. The plant is mutilated, muddy, and half destroyed, yet its resistance remains visible even in ruin. Function: closing image; Hadji Murad becomes the thistle, not as victory but as irreducible vitality.

## 7. KEY SCENE

**Selected treatment scene number: 83 — SHRUB CLUMP / OPEN GROUND — CONTINUOUS**

```screenplay
EXT. SHRUB CLUMP NEAR THE ALAZAN — MORNING

Smoke hangs low among the wet spring leaves.

The ditch is shallow, scraped raw by daggers and hands. Branches, mud, torn grass, spent cartridges.

HADJI MURAD lies half on his side, one hand pressed under his ribs. Cotton from inside his beshmet is dark with blood.

Beside him, ELDAR lies dead. His young face is turned toward Hadji Murad, eyes open, mouth barely parted.

KHANEFI strips cartridges from Eldar’s coat with quick, practical fingers.

KHAN MAHOMA, pale now, still smiles as if the smile has forgotten how to leave him. He loads, sings under his breath, fires.

GAMZALO, one-eyed, blackened with powder, laughs each time a man drops beyond the brush.

Across the shrubs, MILITIAMEN close in from tree to tree.

HADJI AGA stands behind them on higher ground, sword drawn. AKHMET KHAN watches from horseback, tense with inherited hatred.

KARGANOV shouts from cover.

                                    KARGANOV
                        Hadji Murad! Enough. Surrender.

Hadji Murad breathes through his teeth.

He lifts a rifle. Khanefi steadies the barrel for him.

Hadji Murad fires.

A MILITIAMAN drops behind a shrub.

Gamzalo gives a hoarse cry of joy.

                                    GAMZALO
                        There. Another dog has eaten earth.

A volley answers.

Leaves shred. Mud jumps. Khan Mahoma ducks, then laughs softly, ashamed of ducking.

                                    KHAN MAHOMA
                        They shoot like frightened boys.

                                    KHANEFI
                        Load.

Khan Mahoma passes his rifle back. Khanefi takes it and begins loading.

Hadji Murad presses harder into his wound. Blood pushes through his fingers.

Sound narrows.

The battlefield dims around him.

A FLASH: ABU NUTSAL KHAN, cheek severed, holding his face together with one hand while stabbing with the other.

BACK TO SCENE.

Hadji Murad blinks.

A FLASH: OLD VORONTSOV, pale, polished, smiling with eyes that believe nothing.

BACK TO SCENE.

A FLASH: YUSUF, young and proud, turning back at a stream.

A FLASH: SOFIAT in shadow, holding a child close.

A FLASH: SHAMIL’S red beard, half-closed eyes, still as judgment.

BACK TO SCENE.

The sound returns violently.

Eldar’s dead hand slips from the bank into the mud.

Hadji Murad sees it.

He touches Eldar’s wrist once. Not tenderly. As confirmation.

Then he pushes himself up.

                                    KHANEFI
                        Hadji. Stay down.

Hadji Murad takes a pistol from his belt.

                                    HADJI MURAD
                        Give me room.

Khanefi stares at him. He understands.

He shifts aside.

Hadji Murad rises behind the bank. His left leg nearly fails. He grips a branch, steadies, and scans the brush.

A MILITIAMAN rushes forward, sword high.

Hadji Murad fires.

The man folds into the grass.

Hadji Murad throws the empty pistol down and draws his dagger.

                                    KHAN MAHOMA
                        Hadji!

Hadji Murad steps out of the ditch.

For one stunned second, no one fires.

He is bareheaded now. Blood runs down the side of his face. His coat is torn open. He limps forward with the dagger low, not raised, as if walking toward a door only he can see.

                                    HADJI AGA
                        Shoot him!

Gunfire cracks.

Hadji Murad jerks once.

Then again.

He keeps walking.

Another shot hits. His body turns from the force of it. He drops to one knee.

The militiamen surge forward, shouting.

Hadji Murad’s hand finds the trunk of a small tree.

He rises.

The shouting dies.

The men stop.

Hadji Murad stands against the tree, held up by one bloody hand. His eyes are open. Not pleading. Not wild. Present.

Even Hadji Aga hesitates.

                                    AKHMET KHAN
                        Finish it.

Hadji Murad tries to take one more step.

His body shudders.

The dagger falls from his hand.

He pitches forward into the grass.

Silence.

The powder smoke drifts.

No one moves.

Then Hadji Aga advances first, cautious, angry at his own caution.

He reaches the body.

Hadji Murad’s fingers twitch once in the grass.

Hadji Aga raises his dagger and strikes down.

A dull impact.

Hadji Murad’s body does not move again.

Khan Mahoma’s song has stopped.

Gamzalo, wounded and held down by two militiamen, bares his teeth.

                                    GAMZALO
                        You were afraid of him dead.

Hadji Aga looks at him, then kneels by Hadji Murad’s body.

He places one boot on the back, careful where he sets it.

He begins to cut.

Beyond the shrubs, a nightingale starts singing.

Another answers from farther off.
```
