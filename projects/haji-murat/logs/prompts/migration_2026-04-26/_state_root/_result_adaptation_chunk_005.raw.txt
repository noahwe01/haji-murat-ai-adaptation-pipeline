ADAPTATION OUTPUT — CHUNK 005
Hadji Murat | Novel-to-Screenplay | Adaptation Agent

SOURCE PASSAGE SUMMARY:
Hadji Murad and his murid Eldar arrive at the old man's home at night. Hadji Murad disarms
himself and the old man hangs the weapons. They pray together. The old man speaks in proverbs
about Russian violence ("hares and eagles"), deflecting direct news with feigned ignorance.
The murid Eldar is introduced. The scene establishes the code of hospitality, mountain prayer
ritual, and the atmosphere of danger and careful speech under imperial occupation.

SCENE DECISIONS:
- This chunk adapts cleanly into ONE scene (S001): the arrival ritual + veiled conversation.
- No subplot suppression required — this is the inciting spatial grounding of the story.
- The narrator's auktorial voice (NF-04) is dissolved; no voice-over. The weapons on the wall
  and the prayer do the work of establishing the world.
- The old man's proverb ("hares and eagles") is the scene's emotional and thematic peak —
  preserved as the closing beat of dialogue (SC-03: value shift, danger→clarity).

RAW SCENE TEXT (for reference):

EXT./INT. CHECHEN SAKLYA — NIGHT

The yard is dark. A dog knows not to bark. A boy — Sado's Son — stands at the gate, watching
the wood line. Two shapes emerge from the trees: one tall, one shorter. No lanterns. No voices.

Hadji Murad ducks through the low door. The murid Eldar follows. The room is small, clay-
plastered, lamp-lit. Weapons already on the wall: two long rifles and a sword in a painted
scabbard, hanging between basins of hammered copper that catch the light.

The Old Man stands. He doesn't speak. He lifts his hands toward Hadji Murad's burka.

Hadji Murad unslings the burka. Then the rifle. Then the sword. Each piece placed in the old
man's hands without ceremony. The old man hangs them on the nails beside his own — the wall
now a grammar of weapons. He runs his thumb along the blade before releasing it.

Hadji Murad adjusts the pistol at the small of his back. He crosses to the cushions and sits,
pulling his Circassian coat closed. The room is warm. The lamp steadies.

The old man lowers himself to his heels beside Hadji Murad. Both men close their eyes. Both
lift their palms upward. They speak the prayer in unison — brief, spare Arabic syllables —
then draw their hands down their faces, beards caught between joined palms at the last.

A silence. The kind that has been agreed upon.

HADJI MURAD: Ne habar?

The old man does not look at his face. He looks at the breast of his coat, at the place the
rifle was.

OLD MAN: Habar yok.
(a beat)
I live at the apiary. I only came in today to see my son.
(his eyes find the floor)
He knows.

Hadji Murad reads the withholding. He nods once. Asks nothing more.

The door opens. Eldar enters — swift, quiet, reading the room in a glance. He unslings his
weapons exactly as his master did: burka, rifle, sword. Hangs them on the same nails. Takes
the dagger and pistol with him. Sits on the felt mat beside Hadji Murad, legs crossed,
hands loose on his knees.

OLD MAN: (gesturing toward Eldar)
Who is he?

HADJI MURAD: My murid. Eldar is his name.

OLD MAN: (a slow nod)
That is well.

He motions Eldar to a place on the felt. Eldar settles. The old man looks between the two men —
the lamp-light steady on their faces — and then at the wall of weapons that was not there
an hour ago.

OLD MAN: (voice dropping, gravel-dry)
There is no good news.

He pauses. Picks up a small clay cup. Sets it down without drinking.

OLD MAN: The hares have been discussing how to drive away the eagles.
(he draws a breath)
The eagles tear first one, then another.
(a silence)
The other day the Russian dogs burnt the hay in the Mitchit aoul.

He pauses. His jaw tightens.

OLD MAN: May their faces be torn.

The lamp flickers. The weapons on the wall hold still.

Hadji Murad looks at the weapons. Then at Eldar. Eldar has not moved.
