INT. CHECHEN SAKLYA — NIGHT

The room is low-ceilinged, clay-plastered. A single oil lamp burns on the floor. Its flame catches in the hammered copper basins hung along one wall — a dozen small suns in the dark.

ELDAR, Hadji Murad's murid, stands at the door. Young, still. A rifle across his back and a hand already resting on the latch.

Hadji Murad enters first.

He unslings his burka from his shoulders. Sets it aside.

Then the rifle. He lifts it free and holds it level — offering it.

THE OLD MAN receives it with both hands, the way a man receives bread. He runs his thumb along the blade of the bayonet. Then hangs the rifle beside his own on the wall.

Hadji Murad unbuckles the sword. Holds it out.

The Old Man takes it. Hangs it.

The weapons wall grows — Chechen beside Avar, guest's iron beside host's iron.

Hadji Murad adjusts the pistol at the small of his back. Leaves it there.

He crosses to the cushions near the lamp and sits, pulling his Circassian coat closed.

OLD MAN
Ne habar?

A beat.

HADJI MURAD
Habar yok.

OLD MAN
I live at the apiary. I came in today to see my son.

HADJI MURAD
He knows.

The Old Man nods.

OLD MAN
Who is he?

HADJI MURAD
My murid. Eldar is his name.

OLD MAN
That is well.

Eldar does not move.

Both men raise their palms upward, level with their chests.

They speak a prayer — low, brief, in unison.

Then both draw their joined hands slowly down their beards.

OLD MAN (CONT'D)
There is no good news. The hares have been
discussing how to drive away the eagles.

OLD MAN (CONT'D)
The eagles tear first one, then another.

Hadji Murad does not answer. He looks at the lamp.

OLD MAN (CONT'D)
The other day the Russian dogs burnt the hay
in the Mitchit aoul.

OLD MAN (CONT'D)
May their faces be torn.

Hadji Murad lifts his eyes from the lamp and looks at the wall of weapons.

He looks back at the flame.

Eldar has not moved from the door.

Outside, wind moves through dry grass.
