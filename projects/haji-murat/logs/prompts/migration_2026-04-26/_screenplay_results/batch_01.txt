EXT. SUMMER FIELD — EDGE OF HARVESTED RYE — LATE AFTERNOON

A field cut to the horizon. Rye stubble on the right, uncut grass and wildflowers on the left — clover, ox-eye daisies, cornflowers, pale scabious. A man's boots move through the long grass. He carries a loose nosegay.

He stops.

Below, in the ditch: a single crimson thistle, growing upright from the shadow and broken weeds, its flower violent, complete, anomalous.

He looks at it a long moment.

He wraps his handkerchief around his palm. Climbs into the ditch.

He grips the stalk.

It does not yield.

He pulls again — the stalk bends, white fibers stretching, but does not break. He works at it. Methodically. Breaking the fibers one by one until the stalk comes free in his hand, frayed and ugly.

The flower tilts. Already dimming.

He holds the thistle beside the nosegay. It does not belong — the crimson wrong against the soft-colored clover, the broken stalk wrong against everything.

He lets it drop back into the ditch.

He stands above it for a moment, looking down at what he has done. Then he turns and walks away, into the amber rye light.

The ditch and the thistle, diminished, remain.

EXT. BLACK-EARTH FIELD — DIRT ROAD — LATE AFTERNOON

A vast ploughed field. Black earth, uniformly furrowed, unbroken to every horizon. Not a blade of grass. Not a weed. The furrows are precise, total, absolute.

A man walks a dirt road through the center of it.

He stops.

At the roadside: a Tartar thistle. Three-branched. One branch is snapped, hanging at the angle of a broken arm, its flower soiled and pressed into the mud. A second bends under its blackened head, nearly gone.

The third stands upright.

Twisted from the base — a cartwheel has crushed through, torquing the stem sideways. But the stem has not broken. The dark-edged flower at its tip is still open.

The man crouches. He touches the erect stem with one finger.

It does not give.

He withdraws his hand. Straightens. He looks across the ruined field — the uniform black earth, the obliterated furrows, the nothing.

He turns and walks on.

The thistle remains. Alone. Surrounded by mud. Its single upright head the only vertical thing in the world.

Wind moves across the field. The thistle sways. Does not fall.

EXT. MAKHMET AOUL — NARROW SIDE STREET — DUSK

A dry thistle stands at the road's edge — stem broken mid-stalk, head intact, burgundy against grey mud brick. The wind moves it.

A horse rounds the corner from the upland track.

HADJI MURAD — broad-shouldered, lithe even on horseback, a man who holds stillness like a weapon — hooded, burka drawn tight, the barrel of a rifle a dark line beneath the wool. He moves without hurry and without ease. His eyes move constantly; his head does not.

One step behind him, close as a shadow: his MURID, a young warrior whose face is unreadable and whose body is entirely given over to watching.

They hug the left wall. Away from the central square. The sound of the muezzin echo fades, absorbed by the mud-brick.

Hadji Murad's gaze sweeps the length of the side street — doorways, rooflines, the gap between two walls. He reads it the way another man reads a page.

He turns into the street.

The alley narrows. The sound of the square falls away entirely. Only boots and hooves on packed earth.

EXT. SAKLYA ROOFTOP — MAKHMET — NIGHT FALLING

The hillside is terraced with saklyos cut into the slope, their flat rooftops stacked like steps. Hadji Murad stops at the second one from the bottom. The penthouse below is dark — a cold firepit, a coil of rope.

He looks up.

On the flat roof, behind the freshly-plastered chimney: the shape of a man under sheepskin. Still as a stone.

Hadji Murad raises his whip. Taps the chimney rim twice with the handle. Clean, deliberate.

A pause.

The sheepskin shifts. An OLD MAN rises — greasy beshmet, nightcap pushed sideways from sleep, the face of a man who has been woken before by things he would rather not know about. His eyes go left along the alley. Right, up the slope. Back to the figure below.

OLD MAN ON THE SAKLYA ROOF
Alone?

Hadji Murad holds the old man's gaze.

HADJI MURAD
Two.

The old man is still. The question behind his silence is not logistical.

OLD MAN ON THE SAKLYA ROOF
(low, a settling)
Come down from the roof. As Allah wills.

He turns from the edge. The faint creak of a ladder descending.

The alley darkens. Somewhere behind a closed door: the smell of warm bread. Kizyak smoke. The ordinary life that has agreed, without speaking, to absorb the dangerous one.

EXT./INT. SADO'S SAKLYA — NIGHT

The compound is dark. A single clay chimney bleeds smoke into a moonless sky. No lamp in any window.

Hadji Murad reins in at the gate. Before the horse fully stops:

ELDAR — young, square-built, a man whose economy of motion speaks of absolute competence — is already off his horse and at the gate.

On the roof, the old grandfather stirs under his sheepskin. He sits up. He looks at the face below.

He descends backward down the ladder, deliberate, hand over hand, as a man descends who knows what he is descending toward.

Eldar takes the bridle from the old man's hand. Not roughly. Simply. Without acknowledgment in either direction.

HADJI MURAD dismounts. He favors his left leg — a contained thing, the limp of a man who has absorbed the pain so long it has become posture. He ducks under the penthouse.

A BOY freezes in the doorway. Twelve, perhaps thirteen, in a white shirt. His eyes are too wide and he cannot make them smaller.

Inside, the WOMAN OF THE HOUSE moves in the peripheral lamplight, unhurried. She sets three cushions along the front wall.

Between two posts, the master's weapons hang: his rifle, his sword. The old man lifts Hadji Murad's burka from his shoulders. Then the rifle. Then the sword. He adds them to that wall, beside the master's arms.

HADJI MURAD
Selaam aleikum.

OLD MAN ON THE SAKLYA ROOF
Aleikum, selaam.

He turns to the boy in the doorway.

OLD MAN ON THE SAKLYA ROOF (CONT'D)
Run to the mosque. Fetch your father.

The boy does not move for one breath.

OLD MAN ON THE SAKLYA ROOF (CONT'D)
Go.

The boy goes. Bare feet on packed earth, fading into the dark.

SADO'S WIFE
May thy coming bring happiness.

HADJI MURAD
May thy sons live.

The lamp-light shrinks. Outside, the boy's feet on packed earth growing distant.
